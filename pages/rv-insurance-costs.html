How Much Does RV Insurance Cost in 2025?

RV ownership in the United States has surged to record levels, with more than 11 million households owning a recreational vehicle and millions more renting or borrowing them each year. Whether you drive a luxury Class A diesel pusher or tow a modest pop-up camper to state parks on summer weekends, RV insurance is a critical piece of protecting your investment — and in most states, a legal requirement whenever your motorized RV is on public roads.

The average cost of RV insurance ranges from $500 to $2,000 per year for most recreational users, though full-time RV dwellers and owners of large, high-value motorhomes can pay $3,000 to $5,000 annually. The wide range reflects the enormous diversity of RV types — from $15,000 travel trailers to $500,000 Class A coaches — and the equally wide range of how people use them.

This guide covers how RV insurance premiums are calculated, average costs by RV type, the important distinction between recreational and full-time coverage, what RV insurance actually covers, and the best strategies to reduce your annual premium without sacrificing meaningful protection.

How RV Insurance Premiums Are Calculated

RV insurance is priced using a combination of vehicle-specific factors and driver-specific factors. Understanding what drives your premium helps you make smarter decisions when shopping for coverage.

Type and Class of RV

The single most important factor in RV insurance pricing is the type of vehicle being insured. Motorized RVs (Class A, B, and C motorhomes) are treated more like automobile insurance — they require liability coverage and are subject to state minimum requirements for motor vehicles on public roads. Towable RVs (travel trailers, fifth wheels, pop-up campers) are not motorized and therefore do not require separate liability coverage while being towed — they're covered under your towing vehicle's auto policy for liability purposes. However, comprehensive and collision coverage for the towable unit itself requires a separate policy.

Vehicle Value and Age

A brand-new $300,000 Class A diesel pusher costs significantly more to insure than a 10-year-old $40,000 Class C motorhome. Replacement cost coverage — which pays to replace a totaled RV with a comparable new model — costs more than actual cash value (ACV) coverage, which pays the depreciated value. For newer or high-value RVs, replacement cost or agreed value coverage is worth the higher premium; for older units, ACV coverage may be more cost-effective.

Full-Time vs. Recreational Use

How you use your RV dramatically affects your premium. Recreational users who take the RV out on weekends and for a few weeks each year pay significantly less than full-timers who live in their RV year-round. Full-time RV insurance is more like a homeowners policy — it covers personal liability, personal belongings, and provides the kind of continuous protection needed when the RV is your primary residence. Full-time policies can cost 30% to 60% more than comparable recreational use policies.

Location and Storage

Where you live and where you store your RV when not in use both affect your premium. Storing an RV in a locked, covered facility significantly reduces the risk of theft and weather damage compared to leaving it in a driveway or open lot, and many insurers offer storage discounts of 10% to 20%. States with higher auto insurance costs, higher rates of vehicle theft, or exposure to severe weather (Gulf Coast, tornado alley, hail-prone regions) typically carry higher RV insurance premiums.

Driving History and Experience

Motorized RV insurance is closely tied to your driving record. At-fault accidents, DUIs, and moving violations on your record will increase premiums substantially. Conversely, a clean driving record over 3 to 5 years earns meaningful discounts. Some insurers also offer discounts for completing an RV-specific driving safety course — particularly valuable for new RV owners or those upgrading to a larger vehicle class.

Coverage Limits and Deductibles Chosen

Higher liability limits and lower deductibles increase your premium. Selecting a $1,000 deductible versus a $250 deductible can reduce your comprehensive and collision premiums by 15% to 30%. Adding specialty coverages — full-timer liability, emergency expense coverage, campsite liability, or vacation liability — also increases the base premium but adds protection that standard auto policies do not provide.

Full-Time RV Insurance vs. Recreational Use Coverage

One of the most important distinctions in RV insurance is whether you need recreational coverage or full-time RV coverage. Getting this wrong — specifically, carrying recreational coverage while living in your RV full-time — can result in denied claims.

Recreational Use Coverage

Recreational RV insurance is designed for users who take their RV out periodically — weekends, vacations, seasonal trips — but maintain a separate permanent residence. Standard features include:

  • Liability coverage while the RV is being driven or in use at a campsite
  • Comprehensive coverage for theft, weather, and non-collision damage
  • Collision coverage for damage from accidents
  • Vacation liability (campsite liability) for a limited number of days per year
  • Personal property coverage for items inside the RV during trips, up to policy limits

Most recreational policies have a usage limit — the RV must be your primary residence for fewer than 150 to 180 days per year depending on the insurer. Exceeding this threshold without upgrading to full-timer coverage puts you at risk of claim denial.

Full-Time RV Insurance

Full-time RV insurance is designed for people who live in their RV as their primary residence, whether permanently or for extended periods (typically defined as more than 150 to 180 days per year). Full-time policies include additional protections not found in recreational policies:

  • Full-time liability: Broader personal liability coverage equivalent to what a homeowners policy provides — protecting against lawsuits for injury or property damage that occurs in or around your RV, not just while driving.
  • Higher personal property limits: Since the RV contains all your belongings, full-time policies offer significantly higher limits for personal property inside the vehicle.
  • Loss of use coverage: If your RV is damaged and in the shop, loss of use coverage pays for temporary housing while repairs are made.
  • Medical payments coverage: Covers medical expenses for guests injured in or around the RV regardless of fault.

Full-time policies typically cost 30% to 60% more than comparable recreational policies because the exposure is significantly greater — the RV is occupied continuously, personal belongings are more valuable, and liability exposure is ongoing rather than seasonal.

What Does RV Insurance Cover?

Understanding the components of an RV insurance policy helps you assemble the right coverage package for your situation.

Collision Coverage

Collision coverage pays to repair or replace your RV if it is damaged in an accident with another vehicle or object, regardless of fault. Given the size and value of motorhomes, even a minor fender-bender can result in repair bills of $5,000 to $20,000. Collision coverage is optional but strongly recommended for any RV with significant market value.

Comprehensive Coverage

Comprehensive coverage pays for damage to your RV from non-collision causes: theft, vandalism, fire, hail, windstorms, falling objects, flooding, and animal strikes. Hail damage is a particularly common claim for RVers who travel through the Great Plains and Midwest. Comprehensive coverage also typically covers awning damage from wind — a frequent and expensive repair.

Liability Coverage

Liability coverage is legally required for motorized RVs in all states. It pays for bodily injury and property damage you cause to others in an at-fault accident. Standard minimum limits are generally inadequate given the damage a large motorhome can cause — $100,000/$300,000 or $250,000/$500,000 limits are more appropriate for Class A and Class C motorhomes. Umbrella policies can supplement RV liability limits cost-effectively.

Uninsured and Underinsured Motorist Coverage

Uninsured motorist (UM) and underinsured motorist (UIM) coverage protect you when you're hit by a driver who has no insurance or insufficient insurance to cover your damages. Given that approximately 13% of U.S. drivers carry no auto insurance, UM/UIM coverage is valuable protection — especially for RV owners whose vehicles represent significant value and whose repair costs can be substantial.

Personal Belongings Coverage

Standard auto insurance does not cover personal property inside your vehicle. RV insurance typically includes coverage for clothing, electronics, camping gear, and other personal items stored in the RV, up to a policy limit (commonly $3,000 to $10,000). Full-timers should carefully review these limits, as the value of belongings inside a full-time RV is typically much higher.

Roadside Assistance

RV-specific roadside assistance covers towing, tire changes, jump starts, fuel delivery, and lockout service for RVs — but the scale of service required is very different from standard auto assistance. A Class A motorhome requires a heavy-duty tow truck, and finding RV-capable service providers in rural areas can be challenging. RV-specific roadside assistance from providers like Good Sam or coach-net is worth adding, especially for travelers who venture into remote areas.

Vacation Liability (Campsite Liability)

Standard auto liability insurance covers accidents that occur while driving but typically does not cover liability while parked at a campsite. Vacation liability coverage extends your liability protection to accidents that happen in or around your parked RV — if a visitor trips over your awning stakes, for example. Most recreational RV policies include vacation liability coverage for a set number of days per year (commonly 45 to 180 days).

How to Save on RV Insurance

Several strategies can reduce your RV insurance premium meaningfully without sacrificing essential coverage.

1. Bundle with Your Auto or Home Insurance

Most major insurers that offer RV insurance — including Progressive, Nationwide, and National General — offer multi-policy discounts when you bundle RV coverage with your auto or homeowners insurance. Bundling discounts typically range from 5% to 15% on the RV policy and may also reduce your other policies. Get quotes both bundled and separately to confirm the bundled rate is actually lower.

2. Complete an RV Driving Safety Course

Many RV insurers offer premium discounts for completing an accredited RV safety course. The RV Safety and Education Foundation (RVSEF) and Good Sam both offer well-regarded programs. Course discounts typically range from 5% to 10% and the discount often applies for multiple years. For new RV owners, safety courses also provide practical skills that reduce the actual risk of accidents.

3. Choose Seasonal or Storage Coverage

If you only use your RV seasonally — spring through fall, for example — look for insurers that offer seasonal storage discounts or reduced-rate storage coverage during months the RV is not in use. During storage periods, you can typically drop collision coverage while keeping comprehensive (for theft and weather damage) at a significantly lower rate. Some insurers offer policies that automatically adjust coverage and rates based on declared use periods.

4. Increase Your Deductible

Raising your comprehensive and collision deductible from $250 to $1,000 can reduce those portions of your premium by 20% to 35%. This strategy makes the most sense for RV owners with adequate emergency funds to cover the higher deductible in the event of a claim. The annual premium savings often exceed the additional out-of-pocket risk within a few years.

5. Shop at Least Three Insurers

RV insurance pricing varies significantly between insurers. Progressive, Good Sam (underwritten by National General), Nationwide, and Foremost are major players with RV-specific products, but regional and specialty insurers often compete aggressively on price. Comparing quotes from at least three insurers — including one specialty RV insurer — can reveal savings of $200 to $600 per year for equivalent coverage. Use an independent agent who can shop multiple carriers on your behalf.

6. Maintain a Clean Driving Record

The most durable way to keep RV insurance premiums low is to maintain a clean driving record. At-fault accidents can increase premiums by 30% to 50% for three to five years. Defensive driving, adequate following distances — especially important given the stopping distances of large motorhomes — and avoiding distracted driving protect both your safety and your insurance rates.

Frequently Asked Questions

Liability coverage is legally required for all motorized RVs (Class A, B, and C motorhomes) on public roads in all U.S. states, just like standard auto insurance. State minimum liability requirements vary but are generally the same as for passenger vehicles in that state. Towable RVs (travel trailers, fifth wheels, pop-up campers) do not require separate liability coverage while being towed — they're covered under the towing vehicle's policy for liability purposes — but you still need a separate policy for physical damage coverage (comprehensive and collision) on the towable unit itself if you want that protection.

Standard auto insurance typically provides minimal or no coverage for RVs. Your auto policy's liability coverage may extend to a towable RV while it is being towed, but it will not cover physical damage to the RV itself. Motorized RVs (Class A, B, C) generally require a separate RV-specific policy — most auto insurers either exclude motorhomes entirely or offer only very basic coverage. If you have a travel trailer, your auto policy may cover it for liability while towing, but you'll need a separate RV policy for comprehensive, collision, and personal property coverage on the trailer itself.

Class A motorhomes are the largest and most expensive — bus-style vehicles ranging from 26 to 45 feet and valued from $80,000 to $500,000+. They carry the highest insurance premiums due to their value, size, and complexity of repairs. Class B motorhomes (camper vans) are built on van chassis, typically 17 to 24 feet long, and are the most affordable to insure. Class C motorhomes are built on a truck chassis with a distinctive cab-over section, sized between Class A and B, and carry mid-range insurance costs. For insurance purposes, the most important distinctions are the vehicle's value (which drives comprehensive and collision costs) and its liability profile (which is influenced by size and weight).

Most U.S. RV insurance policies automatically extend coverage to Canada with no additional action required. Mexico is different — most U.S. auto and RV policies do not provide coverage in Mexico, and Mexican law requires vehicles operating there to carry a Mexican liability insurance policy. Before driving your RV into Mexico, purchase a Mexican RV insurance policy from a licensed provider. These are widely available online and at border crossings, and typically cost $20 to $50 per day or $200 to $500 for an annual policy depending on the vehicle value.

Renting out your RV through peer-to-peer platforms like Outdoorsy or RVshare is a popular way to offset ownership costs, but your personal RV insurance almost certainly will not cover the vehicle while it is being rented to a third party. Most personal RV policies specifically exclude commercial or rental use. Outdoorsy and RVshare both offer insurance products that cover the vehicle during rental periods. Before listing your RV for rent, contact your insurer to understand your personal policy's exclusions and ensure you have appropriate coverage through the rental platform for the periods the RV is in others' hands.

Actual cash value (ACV) policies pay the depreciated market value of your RV at the time of a total loss — which may be far less than what you paid or what it would cost to replace it. Agreed value policies pay a fixed amount that you and the insurer agreed to at policy inception, with no depreciation applied. For newer or well-maintained high-value RVs, agreed value coverage is significantly more protective. Expect to pay 10% to 25% more for an agreed value policy compared to an ACV policy. Some insurers also offer replacement cost coverage for newer RVs, which pays to replace a totaled unit with a new comparable model.

Coverage for slide-outs and awnings varies by policy and the cause of damage. Awning damage from wind is typically covered under comprehensive coverage — and is one of the most common RV insurance claims. Slide-out damage from mechanical failure is often excluded (it's considered a maintenance issue rather than an insurable event), but slide-out damage from a collision or a covered peril like a falling tree is typically covered. Review your policy's language carefully, as some insurers offer more generous slide-out and awning coverage than others. This is a good question to ask when comparing policies.

Key Takeaways

  • RV insurance costs range from $150 to $3,000+ per year depending on RV type, value, and usage — travel trailers are cheapest, luxury Class A motorhomes are most expensive.
  • Motorized RVs require liability coverage by law in all states; towable RVs are covered for liability under your towing vehicle's auto policy while being towed.
  • Full-time RV living requires a different, more comprehensive policy than recreational use — using recreational coverage as a full-timer can result in denied claims.
  • Bundling with auto or home insurance, completing a safety course, and increasing your deductible are the fastest ways to reduce your premium.
  • Agreed value coverage is strongly recommended for newer or high-value RVs to avoid the sting of depreciation in a total loss.
  • Always get at least three quotes — RV insurance pricing varies significantly between carriers for equivalent coverage.

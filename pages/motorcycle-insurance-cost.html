Motorcycle Insurance Cost Guide: Average Premiums 2026

Michael Torres | Insurance Research Editor | Updated April 14, 2026
Disclaimer: This content is for informational purposes only and does not constitute insurance advice. Motorcycle insurance costs vary significantly by rider age, experience, bike type, and state. Consult a licensed insurance agent before making coverage decisions.

Table of Contents

Motorcycle insurance is legally required in 49 states (Florida exempted for certain riders), yet it remains among the most affordable forms of vehicle insurance in the country. The average full-coverage motorcycle policy costs approximately $700 per year — less than half the cost of the average car insurance policy. Liability-only coverage averages just $250 per year. However, costs can vary enormously: a 19-year-old riding a sport bike in California may pay $2,500+, while a 45-year-old riding a cruiser in Ohio may pay under $300.

Understanding what drives motorcycle insurance costs — and how to keep them low — is the key to protecting yourself on the road without overpaying. This guide covers everything you need to know, including a state-by-state cost table, coverage types, factors that affect your rate, and specific strategies with dollar savings estimates.

Types of Motorcycle Insurance Coverage

Liability Insurance (Required in Most States)

Liability coverage pays for bodily injury and property damage you cause to others in an at-fault accident. Every state that requires motorcycle insurance requires at least liability coverage. Minimum limits vary by state — California requires 15/30/5 (meaning $15,000 per person, $30,000 per accident for bodily injury, $5,000 for property damage), while states like Maine require much higher minimums of 50/100/25.

Financial advisors recommend carrying higher limits than state minimums — at least 100/300/100 — because minimum coverage is typically insufficient to cover a serious accident's actual costs. A serious crash involving hospitalization can generate claims far exceeding state-required minimums.

Collision Coverage

Pays for damage to your motorcycle if you collide with another vehicle or object, regardless of fault. This is essential if your bike is financed (lender will require it) or if the bike's replacement value is significant. Collision coverage is subject to a deductible, typically $250-$1,000. Choosing a $500 deductible instead of $250 can reduce this portion of your premium by 15-20%.

Comprehensive Coverage

Covers non-collision losses including theft (motorcycles are stolen at high rates — over 40,000 per year in the U.S.), fire, vandalism, weather damage, and animal collisions. Given that the average stolen motorcycle is valued at $10,000+, comprehensive coverage is strongly recommended for newer bikes. Motorcycles are stolen at higher rates than cars relative to their numbers on the road, making this coverage particularly valuable.

Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist Coverage

Protects you if you're involved in an accident caused by a driver with no insurance or insufficient coverage. This is particularly important for motorcyclists because injuries tend to be severe and the at-fault driver's minimum liability limits are often inadequate to cover medical bills. Motorcyclists are 29 times more likely to die in a crash than car occupants, making this protection critical.

Medical Payments / Personal Injury Protection

Covers your own medical expenses regardless of fault. Given that motorcyclists face dramatically higher injury risks than car drivers, medical payments coverage provides crucial protection for your own injuries. A typical ER visit after a motorcycle accident runs $5,000-$15,000 — before any surgeries or rehabilitation.

Guest Passenger Liability

Covers passengers riding on your bike if they're injured in an accident you cause. Some states include this in standard liability; others require it as an add-on. If you ever carry a passenger, this coverage is essential. A passenger injury claim can easily exceed $100,000 in medical costs, lost wages, and pain-and-suffering damages.

Roadside Assistance and Towing

Often available as an add-on for $10-$30 per year, roadside assistance covers emergency towing, battery jumps, and fuel delivery if you break down. Given that motorcycles can experience mechanical issues on remote roads, this inexpensive add-on provides valuable peace of mind.

What Affects Motorcycle Insurance Premiums

Motorcycle Type — The Biggest Factor

Sport bikes have powerful engines and are statistically involved in more accidents and thefts. Full coverage on a high-performance sport bike can run $1,200-$2,500+. Cruisers — heavier bikes with lower power-to-weight ratios — are generally considered lower risk and typically cost $500-$900 for full coverage. If minimizing insurance cost is a priority, choosing a cruiser or standard bike over a sport bike is the single most effective step available before you even apply for coverage.

Rider Age and Experience

Young riders — especially those under 25 — pay significantly more for motorcycle insurance, sometimes 2-3x the rates of experienced adult riders. The combination of inexperience and the statistical reality that young riders are involved in more accidents drives these higher rates. A 19-year-old male on a sport bike might pay 3x what a 35-year-old experienced rider pays for the same bike. Completing a Motorcycle Safety Foundation (MSF) course is one of the most effective ways for new riders to lower their premium immediately, typically saving 5-15%.

Riding Season and Geography

States with year-round riding (FL, CA, TX) tend to have higher premiums than states with short seasons (MN, WI, MT). Some insurers offer lay-up discounts for the months a bike is in storage during winter — typically reducing premiums by 15-30% for the off-season months. If you only ride April through October, ask your insurer about this option.

Credit Score

In most states, insurance companies use credit scores as a pricing factor. Poor credit can increase motorcycle insurance premiums by 50-100% over excellent credit. States that restrict credit-based pricing include California, Hawaii, and Massachusetts. Improving your credit score from Fair (580-669) to Good (670-739) can save $50-$150 per year on a typical motorcycle policy.

Riding Record

A single at-fault accident or serious moving violation (speeding 20+ over the limit, reckless driving) can increase your motorcycle insurance premium by 30-60% and follow you for 3-5 years. Multiple incidents can make obtaining coverage difficult. Maintaining a clean record is the most powerful long-term strategy for keeping premiums low.

Storage and Anti-Theft Measures

Where you store your bike matters. A bike garaged at a private residence costs less to insure than one parked on the street or in a shared lot. Anti-theft devices including GPS trackers, disc locks, and alarm systems can earn 2-10% discounts from most insurers and also genuinely reduce your theft risk.

How to Save on Motorcycle Insurance

These seven strategies, applied together, can reduce your motorcycle insurance costs by $200-$700 per year depending on your situation.

1. Complete an MSF Safety Course (Save $35-$150/year)

Most insurers offer 5-15% discounts for completing a Motorcycle Safety Foundation (MSF) Basic RiderCourse or Advanced RiderCourse. The Basic course typically costs $250-$350 and also improves your actual riding skills — a double benefit. For a rider paying $700/year, a 10% discount saves $70/year, and the skills gained may prevent a costly accident entirely.

2. Choose Your Bike Wisely (Save $300-$1,000/year)

A Harley-Davidson Sportster will cost significantly less to insure than a Yamaha R1 of similar purchase price. If minimizing insurance cost is a priority, choose a cruiser, standard, or touring bike over a sport bike. This single decision can save hundreds annually and remains the most impactful choice you make before purchasing coverage.

3. Bundle with Auto or Home Insurance (Save $50-$150/year)

Multi-policy discounts of 5-10% are available from most major insurers when you bundle your motorcycle policy with your auto or homeowners insurance. If you pay $700/year for motorcycle coverage, a 7% bundle discount saves $49/year. Some insurers offer higher discounts of 10-15% for bundling three or more policies.

4. Take a Lay-Up Period (Save $75-$200/year)

If you only ride 6-7 months per year, ask your insurer about a lay-up endorsement that reduces coverage (and premiums) during winter storage months. During lay-up, your comprehensive coverage typically remains in place (protecting against fire, theft, and weather), but liability and collision are suspended. This strategy can reduce annual premiums by 15-30% for seasonal riders in northern states.

5. Increase Your Deductible (Save $40-$150/year)

Moving your deductible from $250 to $500 can reduce your collision and comprehensive premiums by 15-20%. Moving to a $1,000 deductible can save 25-30%. This strategy works best for experienced riders with emergency funds who are unlikely to file small claims.

6. Install Anti-Theft Devices (Save $15-$70/year)

Installing a GPS tracker, disc lock, chain lock, or alarm can earn 2-10% discounts and significantly reduce the likelihood of theft. Given that motorcycles are stolen at high rates — over 40,000 per year nationwide — anti-theft devices provide both a direct financial discount and a practical risk reduction.

7. Shop Around Annually (Save $100-$400/year)

Motorcycle insurance premiums vary significantly between insurers for the same rider and bike. Progressive, Dairyland, Markel, and Nationwide are major motorcycle insurance carriers, and their rates can differ by 30-50% for the same coverage. Get at least 4-5 quotes each renewal period. Riders who shop around consistently pay less than those who auto-renew without comparing.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Carrying State Minimum Liability Only

State minimum liability limits (often 15/30/5 or 25/50/10) are dangerously low for today's medical costs. A serious accident can generate $200,000+ in medical bills and legal judgments — far exceeding minimum limits and leaving you personally responsible for the difference. Carry at least 100/300/100, or better yet 250/500/100, especially if you have significant assets to protect.

Mistake 2: Insuring a New Sport Bike with Full Coverage at a Young Age

A 20-year-old buying a new high-performance sport bike and adding full coverage can easily pay $2,000-$3,500+ per year — more than the bike's annual depreciation. Consider whether the annual premium cost justifies the coverage, especially for bikes that depreciate quickly. An older, lower-powered bike with liability-only coverage might make more financial sense for new riders.

Mistake 3: Not Telling Your Insurer About Modifications

Custom exhaust systems, engine modifications, aftermarket wheels, and custom paint add to your bike's value but are often excluded from a standard policy unless specifically disclosed and added. If your $8,000 bike has $3,000 in custom parts and you don't disclose them, you'll receive only the stock value in a total-loss claim. Always inform your insurer of significant modifications.

Mistake 4: Letting Coverage Lapse Between Seasons

Some riders cancel their policy entirely during winter rather than using a lay-up endorsement. A coverage lapse — even intentional — signals to insurers that you represent higher risk when you return, potentially resulting in higher rates. More importantly, a lapse leaves your stored bike unprotected against theft, fire, and weather damage. Always maintain at least comprehensive coverage year-round.

Mistake 5: Skipping Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist Coverage

Since most states don't require motorcycle insurance as aggressively as auto insurance, and many states have significant uninsured driver populations, UM/UIM coverage is critical for motorcyclists. If you're hit by an uninsured driver and seriously injured, UM coverage pays your medical bills. Skipping it to save $50-$100 per year is a false economy given the severity of typical motorcycle injuries.

Motorcycle Insurance vs. Car Insurance: Cost Comparison

Motorcycle insurance is generally cheaper than car insurance because motorcycles have lower replacement values and repair costs. However, the liability cost per dollar of coverage is not necessarily lower — the risks to others in an accident involving a motorcycle can be just as significant as with a car.

Key Takeaways

  • Average full-coverage motorcycle insurance costs approximately $700/year; liability-only averages about $250/year nationally.
  • Bike type is the single biggest factor — sport bikes cost 2-3x more to insure than cruisers of similar value.
  • Young riders under 25 typically pay 2-3x more than experienced adult riders for the same coverage and bike.
  • Completing an MSF safety course earns 5-15% discounts and genuinely improves riding skills.
  • State minimums are rarely sufficient — carry at least 100/300/100 liability coverage to protect yourself against serious accident claims.
  • Shopping around annually and bundling with other policies can reduce premiums by $150-$500 per year for many riders.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is motorcycle insurance cheaper than car insurance?

Yes, in most cases. The national average full-coverage motorcycle insurance premium is approximately $700/year, compared to about $1,935/year for a car. However, liability coverage for motorcycles isn't necessarily cheaper per-unit of coverage, and the lower total cost largely reflects the lower replacement value of most motorcycles compared to cars. For expensive sport bikes or powerful customs, motorcycle insurance can approach or exceed car insurance costs.

What states require motorcycle insurance?

49 states plus Washington D.C. require at least liability motorcycle insurance. Florida is the exception — it does not require motorcycle riders to carry liability insurance by law, though it does require you to show financial responsibility and most lenders and riders carry it anyway. Even in Florida, if you cause an accident, you are personally liable for damages, making insurance advisable regardless of legal requirements.

Does my car insurance cover my motorcycle?

No. Motorcycles require separate insurance coverage. Your auto policy explicitly excludes two-wheeled motorized vehicles. You cannot legally ride a motorcycle on public roads using auto insurance, and any claims made for motorcycle incidents under an auto policy will be denied. Always obtain a separate motorcycle policy before riding.

How much does insurance cost for a beginner motorcyclist?

Beginners, especially those under 25, typically pay the highest rates. A 19-year-old male new rider on a sport bike in a high-cost state might pay $2,000-$3,500 per year for full coverage. A 25-year-old new rider on a cruiser in a moderate-cost state might pay $600-$1,000. Taking an MSF safety course and choosing a standard or cruiser bike rather than a sport bike are the two most effective strategies for new riders to keep costs manageable.

Can I get motorcycle insurance with a suspended license?

Obtaining motorcycle insurance with a suspended license is very difficult and expensive. Most standard insurers will not write a policy for a rider with a suspended license, as it creates significant legal and liability exposure. Non-standard or high-risk insurers may offer coverage at substantially higher premiums — often 2-3x standard rates. The best approach is to resolve the license suspension before shopping for coverage.

Does motorcycle insurance cover accessories and gear?

Standard motorcycle policies typically do not cover riding gear (helmets, jackets, gloves, boots) or accessories unless you specifically add coverage. Most policies do cover permanently installed accessories up to a limited amount (often $1,000-$3,000). If you have expensive custom accessories or gear, ask your insurer about adding "accessories and safety apparel" coverage for a small additional premium.

Is it worth getting comprehensive motorcycle insurance for an older bike?

Run the math: if your motorcycle's current market value is $3,000 and comprehensive plus collision coverage costs $400-$500/year after your $500 deductible, the maximum you'd ever collect is $2,500. That may not justify the premium. A common rule of thumb is to drop comprehensive and collision when your annual premium exceeds 10% of the bike's value. For a $3,000 bike, if combined collision/comprehensive costs more than $300/year, consider dropping it.
